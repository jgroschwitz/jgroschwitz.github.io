Mapping German Dialect Data

This interactive visualization shows the data of the REDE "Regionalakzente in Deutschland" survey. The survey recorded deviations from standard German pronounciation by speakers across Germany. In other words, the survey describes the landscape of German dialects through a collection of over 5000 datapoints. Each datapoint describes:

  1. the city or town where the sound was observed,
  2. the standard German pronunciation of the sound (standard reference),
  3. how it was pronounced by the local speaker (regional realization),
  4. and optionally some example words where the sound occurs.

In my visualization, I create maps that focus on one sound in standard German and display that sound's regional variation. For example, the figure on the right shows the regional pronunciations of /i/, which is the so called lax i sound of German, used for short i sounds as in the German words "Wind" or "Kissen". All sounds such as /i/ are spelled here using the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA). At each city for which the survey contains data for this sound, the map shows all recorded regional realizations in a pie chart. Similar regional sounds have similar colours (as explained below). Hovering over a segment of a pie chart shows the regional realization, city name and possible examples in a tooltip.

Note that in each pie chart, each datapoint has the same weight. That is, the width of a slice of the pie is simply determined by how often that regional realization was recorded in the survey. Due to the small sample size of the survey, the pie charts should therefore not be interpreted as representing the prevalence of certain sounds in the overall population. Rather, the pie charts simply visualize the survey data.

Data limitation: The survey only recorded deviations from the standard pronunciations. Therefore, there are an unkown amount of occurrences where the local speaker actually used the standard pronunciation (e.g., the sound with standard reference /i/ has the local realization [i]). These occurrences were not recorded in the survey, and are therefore also not present in the visualization.

Analysis

To analyze the data, I consulted with my friend Meaghan Fowlie, Assistant Professor at the Department of Linguistics at Utrecht University. We first looked at the local realizations of /i/, i.e. lax i. One key observation here is that some of the local realizations, i.e. deviations from the standard language, are simlilar to each other: they are deviations in the same direction, just more or less extreme. For example, the sound [y] is produced similarly to the sound [i] (in terms of placement of the tounge etc.), but with rounded lips. The sound "[i] with rounder lips" is inbetween [i] and [y] (i.e. [y] has full lip rounding, and "[i] with rounder lips" has partial rounding). Therefore, "[i] with rounder lips" and [y] are two similar deviations form the standard pronounciations, with [y] simply being a more extreme case.

Another example of this are the sounds "[i] slightly centralized", [ɘ] and [ə]. They are all variants of centralization. When pronouncing the standard sound [i], the topmost part of the tongue, which is the crucial bit for pronunciation of vowels, is near the top and front of the mouth. In "[i] slightly centralized", that topmost part of the tongue is slightly more towards the center of the mouth. In [ɘ], it is even more towards the center and in [ə], it is fully at the center. Thus, these three sounds form another gradient of the same kind of deviation, from slight to strong.

Visualization spotlight: To make the visualization more intuitive and informative, I chose similar colors for similar deviations, using more intense colors for stronger deviations. For example, "[i] slightly centralized", [ɘ] and [ə] are all purple, and the sounds with stronger centralization are a more intense purple. Similarly, "[i] with rounder lips" and [y] are both yellow, with [y] more intense.

One observation here is that often, more extreme deviations are less widespread than more minor deviations. For example, "[i] slightly centralized" is found in many parts of Germany, whereas [ɘ] is rarer, and [ə] is very rare, found only near Berlin. But this pattern does not always hold: "[i] with rounder lips" and [y] are both widespread.

A well known fact from the history of German shows up in this data as well. Note first the prevalence of pronouncing /i/ as [e] (dark red) in northern Germany. These are likely instances of "low German", an old German dialect in the north that used to form a dialect continuum with Dutch. Indeed in Dutch, a similar sound shift occurs: for example, German "Milch" (English: milk) is Dutch "melk".
