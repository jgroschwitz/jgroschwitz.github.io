In Utrecht, is Cycling Faster than Driving a Car?

The Question

While living in Utrecht, I noticed that often when I use Google Maps to plan a trip within the city, the estimated time for travelling by bike is shorter than the estimate for travelling by car. I hadn't noticed that in the other cities I lived in before. I was wondering, did I just not notice this before, or is Utrecht really different from other cities? And is it just the odd route where biking is faster, or is it a real pattern?

The Project

I decided to answer these questions with the power of data. The plan: to sample random addresses in both Utrecht and, as a baseline, another city I have lived in before: Sydney. Then to use a route planner to gain a large sample of routes in both cities. And finally to visualize and analyze the data. The result: driving a car in the Utrecht city center is indeed a very slow method to get from A to B. That's the main result anyway, there's lots more intersting tidbits in the analysis down below, so keep reading!

The Data

I obtained full address lists for both Utrecht and Sydney from OpenAddresses. To get route information, I used the Openrouteservice API. Using a different route planner won't quite answer my questions about my Google Maps, but Google Maps doesn't have a free API to access. I actually ended up setting up my own Openrouteservice server locally (this is possible on a regular desktop computer if you restrict your map data to a small area like a single city). That was its own whole adventure, but detailing it here would get us a bit far off course. Let's look at the results instead!

City Center Results

To compute average travel times in the city centers, and to have comparable distributions of travel distances, I selected a square kilometer in each city's center (which fit conveniently well) and sampled addresses from OpenAddresses within that area. Cause Analysis

So where does this difference come from? There are two possible explanations: first, the route might simply be longer because you have to take a lot of detours. Second, the car may have to drive very slowly. Luckily, we can distinguish between two factors using our route data, because Openrouteservice provides us with not only travel durations, but also route distance (that is, the actual distance driven, as opposed to the straight-line distance). To visualize the impact of the detours, I compute a detour factor that is the length of the actual route divided by the straight-line distance. A detour factor of 1.0 means that we get to travel directly in a straight line, and higher detour factors indicate longer detours. Here are the average detour factors:

We can see that driving by car in the Utrecht city center has a massive average detour factor of 4.2. That is, going from A to B in the city center of Utrecht by car, you need to travel more than four times the distance compared to going in a straight line! And indeed, living in Utrecht, I can confirm that driving with a car here is a nightmare. It's a web of one-way streets, and many roads are reserved for pedestrians, public transport and/or bikes. Going by car in Sydney also has a large, but not quite as large detour factor of 3.0. By bike, you can take much more direct routes on average in both cities (detour factors between 1.4 and 1.7).

The following plot shows average travel speeds in kph.

Openrouteservice estimates faster travel speeds by car than by bike in both cities, but going by car in Utrecht is not as fast as in Sydney. And indeed, walking through the Utrecht center, one can see how narrow roads, speed limits and lots of bike traffic limit how fast cars can drive.

Combining these insights, we can say that the main cause for slow car traffic in the city center is the huge detour factor. While in Sydney, that factor is not quite so high and the faster travel speeds can compensate, in Utrecht the slower travel speeds cannot. The limitations on car traffic in Utrecht are both a result of the narrow streets in the historic center, but also very much by design, see e.g. here. As a result, while many roads are open for cars (at least one way), they see very little car traffic in practice.

City-Wide Results

So my observation turned out to be correct for the city center. But what if we look at the cities as a whole? To answer that question, we must first answer another: what actually is a city? Where does it start and end, or more precisely here: which addresses count as being "in Utrecht" or "in Sydney"? City boundaries like that are notoriously difficult to define. But since the "research problem" here is essentially based on my intuitions in the first place, I found a good criterion to demarkate the cities: I just want to have data that matches my inuitions. There are probably millions of reasons to draw the boundaries somewhere else, but for this analysis, this will do.

For Utrecht, I started with all addresses where the city entry in the OpenAddresses dataset was Utrecht. That already matched my intuitions pretty well, and after filtering out some postcodes, I got the following sample that corresponds pretty well to what I think is the ciy of Utrecht:

For Sydney, this approach does not work, because in the OpenAddresses data, the city label Sydney is only used for some addresses in the city center. All city of Sydney in the larger sense has a whole tapestry of city labels, as you can see here (mouseover the dots to see their labels).

In the end, I went through all of these 100+ "city" labels and decided whether it "feels" like Sydney to me or not. I mostly followed the lines of local government areas to make the whole procedure a bit less arbitrary, but I deviated from that when it conflicted with my intutions, and I also added some filters by lattitude and longitude to deal with outliers. Note that because Sydney is so much larger, I sampled more routes there (20000 routes, compared to the 1000 routes in Utrecht). First we can observe, again because Sydney is so much larger and because we don't limit ourselves to a fixed size square as for the city center, the route data for Sydney includes longer distances that don't occur in the Utrecht data. For the datapoints where we have comparable distances in both cities, we can see that going by bike has comparable travel times in both cities. Going by car in Sydney is the fastest of the four variants. But crucially for my original question, for all but the shortest Utrecht distances, going by car is faster than by bike! This is also confirmed when we look at the average travel durations, normalized by straight line distance:

This result is quite different from the city-center sample, where going by car in Utrecht was by far the slowest. What has changed? Once again, we can look at the detour factors and average speeds:

The biggest change is in the detour factor: for Utrecht it has dropped form 4.2 in the center to just 1.6 for the city overall. Combine that with a solid increase in speed (17.2 to 26.6), and you get much, much faster car travel times. When taking the bike, trends in the same direction exist, but not nearly to this extent.

What does this mean for real life?

Obviously this analysis has a lot of limitations. There are a lot of factors to real life travel times that I did not take into account here, such as traffic jams, the time to find a parking spot (here's a gem for the German speaking audience) etc. I also tried some different online route planners for single routes, and they did not agree on their travel time predictions, so this analysis is certainly not definitive. But it was certainly a fun exercise for me, and I thought it was pretty neat that my everyday experience was mirrored in the data, despite the limitations. And that leaves me with nothing more to say than thank you for reading, and happy cycling, especially if you live in Utrecht :)
